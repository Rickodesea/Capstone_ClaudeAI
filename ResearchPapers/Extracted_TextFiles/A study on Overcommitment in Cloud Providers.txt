Resumo
Jiang Zhi. Um estudo de sobrealocação em provedores de nuvem. Dissertação
(Mestrado). Instituto de Matemática e Estatística, Universidade de São Paulo, São Paulo,
2025.
A computação em nuvem se tornou uma tecnologia essencial no século XXI, amplamente reconhecida
pela escalabilidade, pelo custo-benefício e pela flexibilidade. Apesar de suas vantagens, as ineficiências no uso
de recursos continuam sendo um desafio significativo para os provedores de serviços em nuvem. Por exemplo,
estudos sobre o Google Borg, um sistema de gerenciamento de clusters, mostraram que apenas cerca de 20% da
capacidade computacional e 40% da memória são utilizados, deixando uma quantidade substancial de recursos
subutilizados. Essas ineficiências são geralmente causadas por usuários que superestimam suas necessidades
de recursos e pelas complexidades de gerenciar cargas de trabalho em ambientes imprevisíveis.
Este estudo foca nas políticas de sobrealocação como uma solução para melhorar a utilização de recursos.
A sobrealocação envolve a alocação de mais recursos virtuais do que o hardware físico pode suportar,
aproveitando de forma eficaz a capacidade subutilizada. Gerenciando com cuidado a qualidade de serviço
(QoS), essa técnica pode aumentar a eficiência sem comprometer o desempenho. A pesquisa analisa estratégias
do estado da arte em sobrealocação, introduz uma ferramenta de coleta de dados para obter informações do
traço de cargas de trabalho de provedores de nuvem e desenvolve um simulador para avaliar essas políticas
em diversos cenários de carga de trabalho. O objetivo da dissertação é desenvolver ferramentas para estudar
a sobrealocação, visando minimizar o desperdício de recursos e melhorar o desempenho de ambientes de
computação em nuvem.
Palavras-chave:
Computação em nuvem. Alocação de recursos. Escalonamento. Sobrealocação.


Abstract
Jiang Zhi. A Study on Overcommitment in Cloud Providers. Thesis (Master’s).
Institute of Mathematics and Statistics, University of São Paulo, São Paulo, 2025.
Cloud computing has become a key technology of the 21st century, widely recognized for its scalabil-
ity, cost-effectiveness, and flexibility. Despite its advantages, resource inefficiencies remain a significant
challenge for cloud service providers. For example, studies on Google Borg, a cluster management system,
showed that only about 20% of computational capacity and 40% of memory are utilized, leaving substantial
resources unused. These inefficiencies are often caused by users overestimating their resource needs and the
complexities of managing workloads in unpredictable environments.
This study focuses on overcommitment policies as a solution to improve resource utilization. Over-
commitment involves allocating more virtual resources than the physical hardware can support, effectively
leveraging underutilized capacity. By carefully managing quality of service (QoS), this technique can boost
efficiency while maintaining performance. The research examines state-of-the-art overcommitment strate-
gies, introduces a data collection tool to gather workload trace information from cloud service providers, and
develops a simulator to evaluate these policies under diverse workloads. The goal of this thesis is to develop
tools for studying overcommitment, aiming to minimize resource waste and enhance the performance of
cloud computing environments.
Keywords:
Cloud computing. Resource allocation. Scheduling. Overcommitment.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Cloud computing is a rapidly evolving technology that has transformed the way
organizations and individuals utilize computational resources. This technology enables
users to process, store, and access data at any time, as long as an internet connection is
available.
A foundational concept that enables cloud computing is virtualization, which involves
the creation of a software abstraction over computer hardware to share the processing,
storage, and network devices of a physical machine among multiple users. At the beginning,
virtualization was implemented solely through virtual machines (VMs). VMs are software-
based emulations of a physical computer that allow multiple operating systems to run
simultaneously on a single physical machine. Each VM operates with dedicated resources
managed by a hypervisor, such as Xen (Xen Project, n.d.), KVM (Linux Kernel Virtual
Machine, n.d.), or ESXi (VMware, n.d.).
Over time, more lightweight virtualization methods emerged, functioning similarly to
VMs but without fully emulating a physical computer. These are now commonly known
as containers, with Docker (Docker, n.d.) being the most widely used container platform
and Kubernetes (Kubernetes, n.d.) serving as the leading orchestration tool for managing
and scaling containerized applications across distributed environments.
Several organizations, including cloud service providers (CSPs), have adopted virtual-
ization in their cloud infrastructure. By providing computational resources, CSPs enable
organizations to manage their cloud infrastructure efficiently.
A recurring challenge and major concern for CSPs is resource waste in their infras-
tructure, produced primarily from inefficient resource usage by users and the complexities
of managing workloads in unpredictable environments. In early 2012, studies revealed
low average CPU and memory utilization in Google’s production clusters, at only 20%
and 40% (Reiss, Tumanov, et al., 2012), respectively. At that time, researchers reported
similar findings in Amazon AWS EC2 with CPU utilization averaging between 7% and
17% (Liu, 2011). In 2018, Alibaba’s Sigma and Fuxi had improved their resource utilization
by achieving 38.2% CPU utilization and 88.2% memory utilization, but only considering
online services (Guo et al., 2019). When CSPs improve resource utilization, infrastructure
costs are optimized, reducing waste from underutilized resources.

2
1 | INTRODUCTION
This thesis investigates issues related to inefficient resource utilization in cloud com-
puting environments with a particular focus on the mechanism of overcommitment.
Overcommitment involves CSPs allocating more resources than are available. While this
strategy can increase resource utilization, it also introduces the risk of performance degra-
dation if actual usage exceeds expectations. This thesis also explores how overcommitment
strategies can achieve a balance between optimizing resource efficiency and preserving
the quality of service (QoS).
1.1
Motivation
The primary motivation for studying overcommitment is to improve resource utiliza-
tion within CSPs, as previously highlighted. Idle resources that are allocated but remain
unused represent a significant resource waste for CSPs. By implementing overcommitment
policies, these idle resources can be effectively utilized, leading to more efficient resource
allocation.
The availability of CSP trace data, even in partial form, enables external researchers
to study and gain valuable insights into CSP environments. The data support the design
and testing of new models and policies by simulating how these strategies might perform
in real-world cloud settings. In our research on overcommitment policies, these publicly
available datasets provide a solid foundation for validating the resource allocation strategies
using real-world data (Cortez et al., 2017; Wilkes, 2011; Wilkes, 2020; Ding, 2018).
Overcommitment policies can also be applied in Kubernetes clusters, where clusters can
manage nodes to be overcommitted with pods. Nonetheless, Kubernetes clusters lack built-
in mechanisms for automated overcommitment. Manual configuration of the system can be
time-consuming, especially in dynamic environments with frequently changing workloads.
This requires constant adjustments by administrators, leading to wasted maintenance
time. An automated system capable of overcommitting resources without administrator
intervention could significantly improve administrative efficiency by reducing the time
spent on cluster maintenance tasks (Rzadca et al., 2020).
Another issue contributing to resource underutilization in cloud environments is
that users frequently overestimate the resource requirements for their workloads. The
accumulation of these overestimations results in significant resource underutilization. By
understanding the extent of resource overestimation, overcommitment policies can be
designed to mitigate the amount of unused resources (Rzadca et al., 2020).

1.2
Objective
The objective of this research is to develop state-of-the-art overcommitment policies
for CSPs. These policies are designed to minimize resource wastage by lowering the
number of active machines, reducing QoS constraints to increase resource utilization,
and consequently decrease infrastructure costs. To accomplish this objective, several key
research questions related to overcommitment policies have been established:
(1) RQ1 - What overcommitment policies are currently employed in cloud computing?
(2) RQ2 - What artifacts and metrics can be utilized to evaluate the effectiveness of
overcommitment policies?
(3) RQ3 - How can containers be categorized into classes to optimize the effectiveness
of overcommitment policies?
(4) RQ4 - What approaches can be implemented to automate overcommitment policies?
1.3
Contribution
The main contributions of this thesis are:
(1) Exploration of the state-of-the-art overcommitment policies. The different
types of overcommitment policies employed by CSPs are introduced and explored
in Chapter 2, while a detailed literature review of overcommitment is covered in
Chapter 3.
(2) Filtering the dataset for evaluating overcommitment policies. The dataset
used to study overcommitment policies is explained in Chapter 6.
(3) Development of a simulator to test overcommitment policies. The method-
ology for the experiment is discussed in Chapter 4. The simulator is detailed in
Chapter 5, and the experiment settings and results are presented in Chapter 7.

the information thematically to familiarize readers with the topic and explain the
field.

Chapter 2
Overcommitment
Before diving into the concept of overcommitment, it is important to introduce some
fundamental concepts in cloud computing. After this introduction, the overcommitment
problem is formulated, along with its associated advantages and challenges.
2.1
Cloud computing
Cloud computing is a shift from traditional on-premises computing. Traditional comput-
ing relies on hardware that can be expensive to purchase and maintain. Cloud computing
eliminates the need for users to maintain hardware and instead provides a set of processing,
storage, and networking resources for deploying computer workloads.
The paradigm of cloud computing offers several benefits. Users can scale resources as
their customers demand, modify resources easily over the Internet, save physical space by
eliminating hardware, and reduce costs by avoiding the need to acquire all the hardware
from scratch. These advantages have led several companies to adopt cloud computing
(Carroll et al., 2011).
CSPs offer services to their users that can be categorized into three major types: In-
frastructure as a Service (IaaS) provides users with on-demand access to their resources.
Platform as a Service (PaaS) offers users hardware and software resources needed for
cloud application development. Software as a Service (SaaS) delivers an application
stack as a cloud service, including maintenance and management of everything from the
underlying infrastructure to the application software (Google Cloud n.d.).
A contractual relationship is established between the user and the CSP. The CSP is
responsible for delivering a predetermined level of quality of service (QoS). Failure to meet
these obligations constitutes a violation of the contract. QoS is a metric used to determine
whether the service is operating as specified in the contract. If the provider fails to meet the
QoS requirements, it is considered a violation of the Service Level Agreement (SLA).
The QoS guarantees offered by CSPs are more difficult to violate than those used in
this thesis. CSPs typically offer service uptime guarantees ranging from 90% to 99.99%
of the total operational time (Baset et al., 2012). In contrast, this thesis adopts stricter

QoS guarantees than those typically offered by CSPs. In particular, the QoS requirements
in this study are designed to ensure that the total resource capacity of a machine is not
exceeded.
Large public CSPs, such as Alibaba Cloud, Amazon Web Services (AWS), DigitalOcean,
Google Cloud Platform (GCP), Huawei Cloud, and Microsoft Azure, compete with each
other to attract customers to their platforms. These platforms must offer high-quality
services and a user experience at reasonable prices. This competitive effort drives the
platforms to provide excellent customer service while aiming to reduce costs and energy
consumption to nearly zero (Cohen et al., 2017).
Data from CSPs show that resources are often underutilized. Optimizing these unused
resources could generate significant benefits. Even a 1% increase in resource utilization
across systems operating at a scale of hundreds of millions could result in cost savings
reaching millions. Researchers found that overcommitment results in cost reductions
ranging from 1.5% to 17% in specific settings (Cohen et al., 2017).
Virtualization is a technology that facilitates the creation of virtual instances of com-
puter resources. The most well-known virtualization technologies are virtual machines
and containers. A recent trend in cloud computing is the adoption of containers, which
run lightweight application images directly on the host operating system, unlike virtual
machines (VMs) that emulate an entire operating system. Containers are preferred due to
their lightweight nature.
Overcommitment can occur in two scenarios: CSP settings and Kubernetes cluster
settings. In the CSP scenario, a virtual machine (VM) is allocated to a physical machine (PM).
In the Kubernetes cluster scenario, a pod is allocated to a node, according to Kubernetes
terminology. For consistency in naming conventions throughout this thesis, the term
“container” will refer to either a VM or a pod, while the term “machine” will refer to either
a physical machine or a node. The first scenario corresponds to IaaS, whereas the second
scenario corresponds to either IaaS or PaaS.
2.2
Resources types
The CSP operates an infrastructure designed to support the execution of workloads.
This infrastructure is composed of physical machines, each containing distinct types of
resources, with well-defined interactions occurring among them. This section provides
an overview of the most commonly used resource types. The key resource types of a
physical machine include CPU, memory, disk, network, and GPU. Although additional
types of resources may exist, this section focuses exclusively on the most commonly
utilized resources, and the less commonly used resource types are not addressed.
CPU (Central Processing Unit) is a computer resource for executing instructions and
performing calculations for a system to operate. The speed of a CPU is measured in
gigahertz (GHz) and represents the number of cycles the CPU can perform per second.
Modern CPUs have multiple cores that can handle tasks simultaneously through parallel
computing. Most CPUs also have multiple layers of cache memory (L1, L2, and L3) that
enable instructions to have faster access to frequently used data.

Memory is a computer resource that stores data and instructions to enable quick
access by the CPU. The resource is commonly referred to as Random Access Memory
(RAM). It differs from disks such as hard drives or SSDs because it is volatile, meaning all
stored data is lost when the system is powered off.
Disk is a computer resource that performs the reading and writing of data to persistent
storage. The term “disk” originally refers to storage devices that utilize spinning platters,
such as Hard Disk Drives (HDDs). The term is also used for storage devices without moving
parts, such as Solid-State Drives (SSDs). Disk has several access methods: traditional
read and write operations, memory-mapped I/O (mmap), direct I/O (read/write), and
asynchronous I/O (AIO).
Network is a computer resource used for transferring data between physical machines.
Network metrics are determined by bandwidth, typically represented in megabits per sec-
ond (Mbps), which measures data transfer capacity, and latency, measured in milliseconds
(ms), which is the time required for data to travel between the source and the destination.
This includes the time taken for data to be sent from the source, received by the destination,
and the response to be sent back to the source, often known as round-trip time (RTT).
GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) is a computer resource initially designed as an elec-
tronic circuit for digital image processing and computer graphics. As time has passed, GPUs
have evolved to perform high-speed mathematical calculations through parallel processing,
making them obligatory for tasks such as machine learning, scientific simulations, and
other compute-intensive parallel applications.
The CSP allocates containers with the requested resource types on an available machine.
Each resource type employs a distinct mechanism for overcommitment, determined by
its specific functionality. This thesis focuses on CPU and memory resources because the
trace data are limited to these two resource types.
2.3
Overcommitment
Overcommitment occurs when the total allocated amount of a resource type for con-
tainers exceeds the available capacity of that resource on a machine. In the literature,
overcommitment is also referred to as oversubscription, overbooking, or multiplexing.
On the one hand, when a machine is overcommitted, the containers inside the machine
may experience overload, leading to potential SLA violations. On the other hand, when a
machine is undercommitted, the machine has low resource utilization, resulting in wasted
resources. Low resource utilization can lead to reduced profits, highlighting the importance
of efficient resource allocation to improve both energy and cost efficiency.
A concept analogous to overcommitment in cloud computing can be observed in
other industries, such as the airline industry, where it is referred to as overbooking. The
problem in the airline industry can be described as follows: an airline has a plane with a
fixed number of seats, and each passenger needs a seat during the flight. The objective is
to maximize the number of occupied seats, as unoccupied seats represent wasted capacity
that could have been assigned to paying passengers. At the same time, some passengers
may cancel or fail to board their flight at the last minute.

The goal is to maximize profit while minimizing the occurrence of dissatisfied cus-
tomers who might require compensation, such as hotel accommodations or rebooking on
subsequent flights. This concept has a clear parallel to overcommitment in cloud computing.
Unlike airline seats, resource allocations in cloud computing can often be represented
as real numbers, allowing for fractional allocation and containers consisting of multiple
resource types that the machine must simultaneously accommodate, unlike a seat in the
airline analogy.

Problem formulation
A mathematical model can be formulated for the overcommitment problem. A machine
is defined as an entity with a fixed number of available resources, while a container is
defined as an entity that executes a workload and requires resources. Containers are
allocated to machines, and the objective is to minimize the number of machines used when
allocating container resources within the available machine resources.
The problem is commonly known as vector bin-packing, for which no optimal
solution exists. In this scenario, the CSP manages machines with finite resources, such as
CPU, memory, disk, and network. The provider must assign containers to these machines,
with each container characterized by its specific resources. The objective is to minimize
the total cost, but calculating the cost is challenging because energy consumption depends
on the usage of each resource by the machine. To simplify the problem, the cost metric
is represented by the number of machines used: the fewer machines used, the lower the
cost.
When the number of machines is reduced, containers are packed more tightly on each
machine. This tighter packing can degrade the QoS of individual containers, potentially
leading to performance slowdowns or even termination. A mathematical model similar to
those presented in previous studies (Tchernykh et al., 2020; Cohen et al., 2017) can also
be used to characterize this problem.

2.5
Resource overload
Resource overload occurs when a machine’s usage approaches or exceeds its maximum
capacity. Under these conditions, container performance becomes unpredictable, as each
resource type exhibits different unusual behaviors. For example, when CPU resources are
overloaded and a new CPU-intensive container is allocated, the performance of previ-
ously deployed containers can degrade, potentially causing slowdowns or making them
unresponsive. Similarly, if memory resources become overloaded and a newly allocated
container requests additional memory, the system may resort to swapping memory to disk.
If memory demand continues to exceed available resources, the machine may forcibly
terminate containers due to an out-of-memory (OOM) condition.
To mitigate resource overload, several techniques can be employed, such as stealing,
quiescing, and live migration (Householder et al., 2014). Stealing allows a master VM to
reclaim resources from another VM to prioritize its own operations. Quiescing temporarily
halts a VM’s processes and creates a backup, allowing the VM to be resumed once resources
become available. Live migration involves transferring the VM from Machine A to Machine
B, reducing the resource usage on Machine A without interrupting or rebooting the VM.
Additionally, CPU throttling is used when a container is close to its CPU limit, constraining
the resource usage to prevent overload.
Another issue associated with memory resources, which can contribute to memory
overload, is memory fragmentation. Memory fragmentation occurs when data are stored
in non-contiguous blocks of memory. During periods of resource overload, fragmented
memory may prevent a container from allocating the necessary resources, even when
sufficient total memory is available. Memory resources are structured contiguously, but as
memory is repeatedly allocated and deallocated, the number of contiguous blocks decreases.
Virtual memory partially solves the problem by using paging techniques, which allow
non-contiguous memory allocation (Halbuer et al., 2024; Mansi and Swift, 2024).
2.6
Slack and automation
Resource limits for containers are usually configured manually by developers inside an
organization. Developers often set these limits higher than the actual resource requirements
of the container to prevent scenarios where a container experiences insufficient CPU or
is terminated due to an OOM condition. The accumulation of these containers creates

11
extra slack in unused resources. This slack results in resource wastage, as it prevents the
placement of additional containers that could otherwise utilize these excess resources
(Rzadca et al., 2020).
Developers may need to adjust resource limits as a container’s requirements change
during the project. Manually reconfiguring these limits consumes valuable developer
time that could be better spent on other tasks. Toil is a metric that quantifies the human
execution time required to perform a task (Rzadca et al., 2020).
Google Autopilot presents a model that performs nearly automatic resource allocation.
Developing a similar software solution for automatic resource allocation could significantly
reduce the toil in cloud container management systems, such as Kubernetes clusters, by
minimizing the need for manual configuration.
2.7
QoS reduction
After utilizing the unused resources from the extra slack, another possible optimiza-
tion involves decreasing the QoS by throttling the CPU. This approach reduces unused
resources but increases the total completion time (makespan). Containers host various
types of applications, each with different requirements. For batch tasks where QoS is
less critical, degradation can be applied without significant consequences. In contrast,
for latency-sensitive services, QoS degradation can negatively impact user experience,
potentially leading users to abandon the service or switch to competitors. Ensuring that
each container operates efficiently on the CSP’s infrastructure is essential for maintaining
user satisfaction.
Defining effective policies for container allocation is crucial for minimizing resource
waste. CSPs must gain a deep understanding of each container’s specific characteristics and
requirements to ensure user satisfaction and optimize costs. This task becomes increasingly
complex due to the high number of containers, and identifying those suitable for QoS
reduction is particularly challenging.
2.8
Overcommitment policies
Overcommitment policies require detailed information about containers. When a
container is first assigned to a machine, its behavior and resource usage patterns are
unknown. In such cases, the container is assumed to use the total allocated resources. The
standard approach to this uncertainty is to adopt a conservative strategy. Over time, as
the container runs and metrics are collected, its behavioral patterns become clearer. Once
sufficient data are gathered, it becomes theoretically possible to identify and implement
an overcommitment policy tailored to the specific requirements and behavior of the
container.
Overcommitment policies can be categorized into machine-centric overcommitment
and container-centric overcommitment.

2.8.1
Machine-centric
Machine-centric overcommitment occurs when a machine’s physical resources are
overcommitted by creating additional virtual resources, extending the total resource
capacity beyond the actual physical limits. For example, if 100% of a machine’s physical
resources are allocated, adding an extra 20% of virtual resources increases the total capacity
to 120%.
This strategy improves resource utilization because users frequently overestimate their
resource requirements, leaving a significant portion of the allocated resources unused. The
accumulation of these unutilized resources creates additional capacity that can be leveraged
to allocate new containers, making it feasible to assign additional virtual resources.
An example of this approach is implemented by Microsoft Resource Central, which
defines quotas for CPU overcommitment. In this approach, an additional 15% of virtual
cores are allocated beyond the physical core capacity, effectively increasing the total
resource capacity of the machine by 15%. This practice is exclusively applied to machines
used for internal development. CSPs typically adopt a more conservative approach when
managing users’ containers to ensure system reliability and user satisfaction (Cortez
et al., 2017).
2.8.2
Container-centric
The container-centric approach involves reducing the resource limits for each container.
By lowering these limits, more containers can be allocated to a single machine, thereby
decreasing the total number of machines used.
The overcommitment rate employed by CSPs for their machines is usually based
on the p-th percentile of resource usage or a predefined usage percentage interval. The
overcommitment rate often depends on the type of task being executed. For batch tasks that
do not require high QoS, containers can be overcommitted more aggressively. Conversely,
for services, the overcommitment rate must be lower to avoid negatively impacting latency.
There are two primary approaches to container overcommitment: static overcommitment
rates or dynamic overcommitment rates.
Static
Static overcommitment is a standard method in which a fixed overcommitment rate is
applied equally to all containers. This straightforward approach is widely implemented,
including in systems like Google Borg (Bashir et al., 2021).
Microsoft Resource Central applies overcommitment exclusively to non-production
VMs. For these VMs, the overcommitment rate is set based on the 95th percentile of
resource usage for each container (Cortez et al., 2017).
Dynamic
Dynamic overcommitment adjusts the overcommitment rate based on the workload of
each container. In Google Autopilot, the overcommitment rate is determined by the type

policy ensures efficient utilization of CPU resources for non-latency-sensitive tasks while
satisfying reserved time constraints (Kedia and Makagonov, 2018). Memory allocation
follows the standard 99th percentile (P99) of memory usage.

Literature review
A regular cloud is a cloud environment where resources such as network and disk I/O
can be overcommitted. An overcommitted cloud takes a step further by also allowing
CPU and memory to be overcommitted, in addition to the already overcommitted network
and disk I/O (Baset et al., 2012).
In a regular cloud, network and disk I/O resources are typically multiplexed among
containers. Multiplexing is a mechanism that combines two or more signals or data
streams from multiple channels into a single channel, integrating the channels among
containers and allowing the same data stream to be used. The primary goal of an overcom-
mitted cloud is to enable the overcommitment of CPU and memory resources as well, but
these two resources are not as easy to overcommit as network and disk I/O and require
other elaborate techniques to achieve overcommitment.
Using overcommitment presents an ethical dilemma: balancing the risk of being unfair
to clients against the potential benefits of conserving resources that would otherwise
go unused. If overcommitment significantly reduces the clients’ QoS, it raises serious
concerns about fairness and service reliability. Conversely, if it does not negatively affect
QoS and successfully optimizes resource usage, it can be considered a responsible and
efficient method to reduce resource wastage.
In an overcommitted system, security vulnerabilities can arise from improper isola-
tion between containers hosted on the same machine. These vulnerabilities may lead
to information leakage, where sensitive data from one container becomes accessible
to other containers sharing the same machine. Such breaches pose significant risks, as
sensitive information intended solely for the data owner may inadvertently be exposed,
compromising the system’s confidentiality and integrity (Kim et al., 2014).

The overcommitment strategies used in open-source cloud solutions are outdated.
OpenStack, an open-source cloud solution created in 2010 by Rackspace and Anso Labs,
uses default overcommitment ratios of 16:1 for virtual CPUs to physical CPUs and 1.5:1
for virtual RAM to physical RAM (OpenStack, n.d.). The ratio x:y indicates that for every

In comparison, VMware vSphere provides a guideline for a 6:1 ratio of virtual CPUs
to physical CPUs and a 1.5:1 ratio of virtual RAM to physical RAM. These values were
established at a time when servers and codebases were not optimized for efficient CPU
utilization. As the cloud landscape has evolved, new recommendations have emerged to
reduce these ratios to 3:1 for virtual CPUs to physical CPUs and 1.25:1 for virtual RAM to
physical RAM (Cloud Architect Musings, 2013).
The parameters used in both OpenStack and VMware can be considered outdated, and
their overcommitment strategies need to be modernized to keep pace with advancements
in hardware and software optimization. In Red Hat Enterprise Linux, a service named
“Tuned” is available, which employs a mechanism for setting resource usage profiles for
resource optimization but does not allow for parameter adjustments.

3.1
Cloud service provider
Google Cloud (Rzadca et al., 2020), Microsoft Azure (Cortez et al., 2017; Kumbhare
et al., 2021), and Alibaba Cloud (Z. Zhang et al., 2014; Guo et al., 2019) have been researched
using cloud traces, analyzing how CSPs use the data to make scheduling and overcommit-
ment decisions. These studies demonstrate how information about cloud environments is
utilized, and such insights can help define effective overcommitment policies.
Google Cloud uses Autopilot, an automated task deployer designed to reduce the
human toil required to configure resource needs. Google Autopilot employs an algorithm
similar to reinforcement learning to allocate containers to machines. During system spikes,
the algorithm uses a half-life technique to gradually adjust the container resource sizes,
decreasing them over time. To monitor and manage spikes, the system calculates a lower
limit and an upper limit to track its performance (Rzadca et al., 2020).
Microsoft Azure uses Resource Central, which employs a machine learning algorithm
to predict the resource utilization and differentiate between human and machine containers.
In the infrastructure of CSPs, a more conservative approach is adopted for resource usage.
Resource Central overcommits resources only to machines intended for development
purposes (Cortez et al., 2017).
Alibaba Cloud allocates batch tasks during night periods when CPU usage of services
is lower, allowing batch tasks to utilize unused resources (Guo et al., 2019).
3.2
Resource optimization
For disk I/O and network oversubscription, resources are multiplexed among the
containers.
Memory oversubscription techniques include ballooning/reclamation, a method that
allocates idle or unused memory pages for use. Compression is another technique where
memory pages are compressed and stored on disk, though it has the drawback of requiring
decompression later. Swapping is also a method that moves random memory pages to disk
to free up physical memory. Ongoing research continues to explore and optimize these
techniques to improve their efficiency and performance (Wang et al., 2023).
Batch tasks can be scheduled alongside online services, utilizing CPU resources when
resource usage from online services is low (Guo et al., 2019). Another innovative approach
to leveraging underutilized resources involves using GPU resources during the idle phases
of deep learning processes to perform image rendering for live gaming services (W. Zhang
et al., 2022).

Overcommitment
For overcommitment, Datos separates jobs into two types of queues: a normal queue
and an overcommitted queue. The normal queue executes jobs at standard priority, while
the overcommitted queue assigns lower priority to its jobs, throttling them to prioritize
the normal queue based on the host machine’s overload level (J. Chen et al., 2018).
QoS reduction can also be applied without using queues, as in Datos, by directly

satisfaction if the product experience deteriorates to the extent that customers abandon
the system.
Several overcommitment policies are explained in prior research (Bashir et al., 2021).
In the terminology used, these policies are referred to as peak predictors. The Borg-default
predictor calculates the sum of container allocations and applies a fixed rate to the total sum,
aligning closely with the machine-centric overcommitment method. The Resource Central-
like predictor calculates the sum of container allocations based on the 95th percentile,
similar to the container-centric static overcommitment method. The paper also introduces
an N-sigma predictor that uses a Gaussian approximation to estimate the resource usage
(Janus and Rzadca, 2017). Additionally, studies have explored overcommitment in the
context of CPU usage distributions for containers. In these studies, container CPU usage
is modeled using either a truncated Gaussian or a Bernoulli distribution. Synthetic data
are generated based on these models, and then a fixed overcommitment rate is applied to
run the experiment (Cohen et al., 2017).

tested vector bin packing algorithms across various scenarios and have introduced the
Vectorpack library, specifically designed for evaluating these algorithms (Mommessin
et al., 2025).
3.5
Overload mechanism
Overcommitting resources can significantly impact the performance of a machine.
When resources are heavily utilized, the machine may become overloaded. In this state,
where the resources operate close to their maximum capacity, tasks running on the
overloaded machine frequently experience performance degradation.
When a machine becomes overloaded, several mitigation strategies can be implemented.
One approach is live migration, which moves containers from one machine to another
to balance the load (Forsman et al., 2015; Jiang et al., 2021). Another method is resource
throttling, which restricts container resource usage to mitigate overload conditions. In
extreme cases, terminating a container may become necessary to free up critical resources
and maintain normal operation for the remaining containers (Rzadca et al., 2020).

Methodology
This chapter describes the research methods used to study overcommitment policies,
always focusing on the research goals and questions. The research goals involve under-
standing how these policies can support CSPs and cluster managers in the development of
more efficient resource utilization while also creating an efficient artifact that provides a
fast feedback loop for experimentation.
The goal of overcommitment policies is to reduce costs by minimizing the number of
machines used and decreasing the number of SLA violations. Several artifacts can be used
to test these overcommitment policies. These artifacts for evaluating algorithms or policies
in experiments can be separated into: In-situ, Simulation, Emulation, Benchmarking, and
Formal Methods (Calheiros, 2010).
“In-situ”: The term originates from Latin, meaning “in the original place”. This method
involves conducting experiments directly in the real environment. Using this approach
requires careful consideration of potential risks, as a crash during the experiment could
affect other programs running on the same machine, which can be problematic if those
programs serve real clients.
Simulation: This method involves creating a virtual environment that replicates the
mechanisms of a real environment using a model, allowing systems to be tested, broken,
or modified without risking the real infrastructure.
Emulation: This method replicates processes step by step, matching how each process
occurs in a real environment while running in a virtual-controlled setting. This approach al-
lows testing mechanisms that the simulation may not capture, showing effects in processes
that arise from the system’s internal operations.
Benchmarking: This method utilizes a collection of programs intended to evaluate
the execution of a cloud computing environment.
Formal methods: These methods apply a rigorous mathematical formulation for the
specification, development, and verification of software and hardware systems.
The approach of utilizing artifacts in experiments extends to cloud computing settings,
where each artifact is evaluated using predefined attributes. The effectiveness of each

Chapter 5
Cloud simulator
A cloud simulator is a tool designed to replicate the cloud computing environments,
reproducing the behavior and functionality of a real cloud system. It enables researchers and
cloud administrators to model and analyze cloud infrastructure in a controlled environment,
providing insights into cloud performance statistics under specific scenarios.
The simulator offers the advantage of testing a cloud environment without the need to
deploy actual cloud resources. It allows researchers and cloud administrators to simulate the
execution of services and applications inside a container without real cloud infrastructure.
As a result, users can experiment and analyze system behavior without incurring the costs
or risks associated with infrastructure failures.
5.1
Comparison of different cloud simulators
In the cloud simulation space, CloudSim is the most popular and widely used cloud
simulator among researchers and is an open-source framework written in Java, designed
to simulate events in a cloud environment. CloudSimPlus is another important simulator
with a simpler syntax and more efficient performance than CloudSim.
CloudSim offers a range of features for running cloud simulations and benefits from a
growing community that continuously improves and expands its capabilities (Andreoli
et al., 2024). Despite these advantages, CloudSim also has limitations. Some features, such
as several overcommitment policies and overload handling, are not supported to my
knowledge.

5.2
Clovers — cloud overcommitment simulator
Clovers is a cloud simulator based on discrete-event handling, implemented in the Rust
programming language as part of a thesis exploring overcommitment policies. Introduced
in 2015, Rust focuses on memory safety to minimize bugs and avoid undefined behavior
through language design. Memory safety in Rust is enforced by eliminating uninitialized
variables, preventing null pointers, and enforcing strict mutability rules through compile-
time verification.
Benefits of Rust include strong memory safety guarantees and a powerful compiler
capable of detecting complex issues, such as data races and buffer overflows. Rust also has
certain drawbacks, such as longer compilation times, a relatively steep learning curve, and
minor limitations associated with its trait system.
CloudSim, the most popular cloud simulator, is implemented in Java. The Java language
offers several advantages, such as an extensive ecosystem and platform independence
across operating systems. There are also drawbacks related to the Java Virtual Machine
(JVM), such as high memory allocation and reliance on garbage collection, which can
negatively impact performance. Furthermore, application execution on top of the JVM
may introduce additional latency. Other concerns include potential null pointer exceptions,
which can cause unexpected program termination, and out-of-memory errors resulting
from inadequate memory allocation.
5.3
Components of the simulator
The cloud simulator consists of the following components:
1. Engine: The engine is the heart of the simulator and processes cloud events at every
timestamp. Events include tracking resource usage for overload management and
handling the insertion and deletion of containers and machines.
2. Overcommitment Policy: This component manages resource overcommitment.
The static policy applies the same p-th percentile value across all containers or
machines, while the dynamic policy adapts the p-th percentile based on container
type, using a higher value for batch tasks and a lower value for services.
3. Scheduler: Responsible for allocating virtual resources such as CPU and memory
to containers, while determining appropriate machines to host each container.
4. Overload Handler: Detects resource overload conditions and communicates these
events to the engine. The engine processes these events and performs appropriate
simulation actions, such as throttling or terminating processes.
5. Evaluator: Measures the results of an experiment on the simulator.
6. Resource Usage Interface: Provides graphical representations of resource usage
metrics, helping users to visually interpret machine and container resource usage.

Scheduler
The scheduler assigns containers to machines based on defined criteria to determine
the placement of each container. Key considerations for scheduling are discussed in
(Bittencourt et al., 2018), and similar principles and challenges apply to overcommitment
policies in cloud scheduling. The scheduler addresses tasks using a vector bin-packing
approach.
5.6.1
Scheduling algorithms
Container scheduling is processed in the order of its allocation times. To assign con-
tainers to machines, various heuristics can be employed, each targeting specific objectives.
The primary goals are to minimize SLA violations and reduce the number of machines
utilized.
To minimize the number of machines used, the problem focuses on tightly packing
machines to maximize the utilization of all available resources. Before introducing the
scheduling algorithms, it is essential to establish some key definitions:
• Vector of machine availability: Calculated as the difference between the machine’s
total resource vector and its resource usage vector.
• Vector of container peak usage: Calculated by taking the maximum value across
each resource dimension in the container’s usage vector.
The scheduling algorithms implemented in the simulator are as follows:
First-fit: The scheduler selects the first machine with sufficient available resources
and allocates the container to it. A variation, First-fit Decreasing, assigns a score to each

machine with sufficient resources.
Best-fit: The scheduler uses a heuristic that sorts machines by their available resources
and allocates the container to the machine with the smallest available resources that can
still allocate the container. For multiple types of resources, the scheduler calculates a
score as ∑𝑖𝑤𝑖⋅(𝑎𝑖(𝑥) −𝑑𝑖(𝑣)), where 𝑎𝑖(𝑥) is the availability of resource 𝑖on machine 𝑥,

L2 norm / Euclidean distance: This heuristic calculates the distance between the

norm (e.g., L2 norm or Euclidean distance) rather than using the dot product (Panigrahy
et al., 2011).
5.7
Overload handler
When a server operates under high utilization, it may encounter sudden spikes in
resource usage. These spikes can be triggered by various factors, such as temporary
hardware failure or a sudden Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack, resulting in a
sharp surge in incoming requests.
The optimal solution is to allocate additional resources to latency-sensitive workloads,
ensuring that servers do not experience performance degradation due to insufficient
resources. Alternatively, implementing an overload handler mechanism can effectively
manage high-load situations.
In the simulator, when a machine becomes overloaded, specific tasks must be either
throttled or terminated to mitigate the load. The simulator prioritizes terminating tasks
with highest resource comsuption to reduce the overall machine usage. While alternative
strategies, such as live migration, are available for handling overload, this thesis focuses
on the overcommitment policy by prioritizing throttling and termination mechanisms
commonly employed in real cloud environments (Hadary et al., 2020; Rzadca et al.,
2020).
In real environments, overload policies for containers are typically separated into two
categories: soft limits and hard limits. Furthermore, different resources often require
unique mechanisms for managing overload, as summarized in Table 5.1.
In a simulated environment, a new type of overload policy can be introduced: no
limits. Under this policy, no containers are terminated during overload situations, and
instead the evaluator is notified about the issue.

For CPU hard limit, the throttle mechanism requires a propagation model to manage
unused CPU resources, since any unused CPU capacity must be carried forward to the
next time step. Two methods are proposed for handling this propagation:
1. Accumulation Method: All unused CPU resources are accumulated and fully
propagated to the next time step.
2. Queue Method: A queue stores unused CPU resources, with only the first value
from the queue propagated to the next time step.

of resource limits. The simulation collects information, ensuring that, at every times-
tamp, each machine in the cluster reports whether container or machine violations have
occurred.

The percentile represents the value below which a specified percentage of the data

For calculating the percentile of resource usage, two approaches are utilized:

The algorithm adjusts the time window by shifting both its start and end positions

decrementing the count at the position being removed.
To calculate the p-th percentile, the algorithm offers two approaches:
1. Using a query to find the position: The algorithm can utilize the third type of
query to directly locate position k in the data structure.
2. Binary search on the value X: Alternatively, a binary search can be performed
on the value X, leveraging the second query that calculates the sum for the interval

for the value at which the cumulative frequency exceeds the specified percentile
threshold.
Alternative approach
An alternative approach that avoids complex data structures, such as priority queues
and segment trees, involves reducing the frequency of p-th percentile calculations by
performing them at predefined intervals (e.g., every 6 hours). This approach utilizes a
deque to store data within the specified time window. When a p-th percentile calculation
is required, the values in the deque are copied into a new vector, sorted, and the p-th
percentile is calculated.
In contrast to the first two approaches, the alternative approach recalculates the p-th
percentile after a fixed interval, using the complete resource usage history. If recalculations
are frequent, this approach becomes impractical.

Data collection
To perform overcommitment experiments, trace data from CSPs are required. These
trace data contain detailed information about the internal environments of the CSPs,
including container and machine resource usage across CPU and memory. Since the
infrastructure of each CSP differs, the types of information provided also change. The
information about each of these CSPs must be completely understood and analyzed to
extract what is useful in the experiment.
6.1
Trace data source
The open-source trace data used in the research come from Google Borg, Microsoft
Azure, and Alibaba Cloud. The trace data provided by these CSPs are only partially available
due to privacy concerns. Full disclosure of the data could potentially be exploited, leading
to issues such as competitive risks and the leakage of users’ private information (Reiss,
Wilkes, et al., 2012). A similar issue occurred with other datasets, such as the Netflix
Prize dataset, in which researchers demonstrated that it was possible to extract users’
information even from anonymized datasets (Narayanan and Shmatikov, 2007).
Each CSP uses a unique data format and type of information in its trace data. The
following sections explore the details provided by each CSP and also suggest an alternative
method for generating trace data using a synthetic workload.
6.1.1
Google Borg
Google Borg Cluster is a cluster management system that handles hundreds of thou-
sands of jobs from thousands of applications across multiple clusters spread worldwide,
each comprising up to tens of thousands of machines. The datasets generated by these
clusters provide information at a terabyte scale.
The Borg cluster is used exclusively for internal Google products and services. As
the predecessor to Kubernetes, Borg introduced key ideas and features that were later
integrated into Kubernetes. More details about the Borg system are available in its original
paper (Verma et al., 2015).

6.2
Container classification
This section looks at RQ3 from Chapter 1, which investigates how containers can be
categorized into classes to optimize the effectiveness of overcommitment policies. The
thesis did not provide a solution to the problem of segmenting and clustering workloads.
Google Autopilot emerged as the only approach that showed potential for further research.
Moreover, other methods lack sufficient container-specific information, making it difficult
to derive meaningful results.
Segmentation involves dividing data into groups based on similar characteristics.
Grouping containers by similar behaviors can help the system predict and generate custom
overcommitment policies for each group. One possible approach is to analyze container
data in greater depth by collecting detailed information, such as the operating system and
container-specific attributes, and then generating decision trees. However, the data are
not publicly available (Cortez et al., 2017).
Clustering is a technique for grouping data based on similarity. Clustering trace time
series is particularly challenging because time series from the same container class can
display distinct shapes (Kedia and Makagonov, 2018). Even within the same workload,
time series patterns can change significantly. As a result, algorithms such as Dynamic
Time Warping (DTW) or K-shapes (Paparrizos and Gravano, 2016) may not perform
well in these scenarios.
When a container trace exhibits a periodic pattern over time, this behavior can be
exploited. An algorithm based on the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) can identify the posi-
tions in the time series that are most likely to correspond to period repetitions. Microsoft
Resource Central leverages this information to distinguish between human and machine
containers. In this context, a human container is defined as a container that interacts with a
human and can be affected by latency, while a machine container is a container used solely
for processing information. Although FFT can find the approximate period of repetition,
distinguishing between human and machine containers remains challenging due to the
complexity of interpreting the trace data in some cases (Cortez et al., 2017).

6.3
Implementation
This section describes the data collection process and its implementation. The source
code is available at https://www.gitlab.com/cloudsimrs/trace-data.
6.3.1
Tools
To accelerate data processing speed through parallelism, the data collection process
takes advantage of two primary tools: Polars and Rayon.
Polars
Similar to Pandas, Polars is a Rust-based high-performance data manipulation library
that efficiently processes large datasets through multi-threading. It offers APIs for both
Python and Rust, often providing improved speed and lower memory usage for large-scale
data processing. The developed code uses Rust’s native DataFrame functionality directly,
ensuring optimal performance.
As a relatively new and rapidly evolving library, Polars experiences frequent syntax
changes, such as updates to the ‘partition_by’ function and DataFrame read formats, which
have required modifications to the code throughout the development process.
Rayon
Rayon is a Rust library for parallel processing, designed to enable concurrent data
processing by efficiently distributing workloads across multiple CPU cores.
6.3.2
Data extraction process
The process begins with downloading the trace dataset. After the download, SHA256
checksums are applied to the files to verify their consistency. Once the integrity of the
files is confirmed, the dataset is processed to extract important information.
Information about containers and machines is collected, including details on CPU
and memory allocation and time series data on CPU and memory usage. Additionally,
the relationship between containers and machines, along with information about the
container’s owner, is available.
An optimization in data reading is batch processing. Instead of reading each file
individually, multiple files can be merged into a single file for processing information in
bulk. This approach minimizes the overhead associated with file operations and improves
read performance.
The container and machine IDs must be parsed and converted to integers to optimize
query performance, as querying by IDs with non-integer formats is inefficient. A hash
table should be created to map these values to their corresponding integer representations,
and the container and machine IDs need to be converted to integers.
After segmenting the container and machine information, the experiment generation
process begins. To ensure consistency across runs of the same experiment, a fixed seed

downloading, an extractor separates the cluster data by machine ID for each JSON shard
using Polars and the partition_by function, converting the data into a Parquet file. The
processed data are then handled on the university server, Brucutu VI, which requires about
one week to complete the task. To transfer data between Brucutu VI and the external hard
drive, Google Cloud Storage (GCS) is used as an intermediary storage solution.
Brucutu VI is one of the processing servers at IME-USP, with its hardware specifications
detailed in Chapter 7. The server operates under specific rules: only half of the available
threads may be utilized, and tasks that violate this rule are terminated by the server
administrator. Due to the large size of the dataset, the data extraction algorithm is divided
into two phases. The first phase, the partitioning phase, partitions the data by machine ID,
and the second phase merges the data by machine ID.
Partitioning phase
The first phase is the partitioning phase. Initially, the cluster data are unstructured
and not separated. During this phase, the data are organized and partitioned based on the
machine ID (machine) and the task ID (container).
For each JSON shard, the extractor generates a list of machine IDs extracted from the
shard. This list is used to identify inconsistencies in the data. Each machine ID is associated
with a list of JSON shard IDs, and the JSON shard files corresponding to each machine ID
are joined together. To enable rapid queries, the extractor also creates a reverse mapping
for each machine ID, linking it to the JSON shard IDs used to generate its file.
Subsequently, for each machine ID, the extractor partitions the data into lists of con-
tainers. These tasks are then separated and processed in parallel using Rayon’s scoped
threads.
Merging phase
The merging phase separates the data based on machine IDs. This process generates
time series information for each machine and produces a list of containers, preparing the
data for simulation. Before running the simulation, the container information for each
machine must be sorted. The data itself isn’t merged but remains separated, as merging
takes time and increases the size of the data to be processed.
The extracted information includes time series data for running processes. The data
are segmented into 5-minute intervals, and any gaps between data points are filled using
the most recent value.

6.4
Summary
The chapter presented datasets from different CSPs, such as Google Borg, Microsoft
Azure, and Alibaba Cloud. Each dataset has specific characteristics and limitations re-
garding format and information. The Google Borg dataset provides only normalized CPU
and memory usage data. The Microsoft Azure dataset categorizes containers into human
and machine workloads, but lacks information about the memory usage time series. The
Alibaba Cloud dataset is limited by restricted time intervals and incomplete data due
to collection issues, and the dataset does not have time series information about batch
tasks, only online services. As an alternative to datasets, synthetic workloads can be used.
Benchmarks such as SDBBench, TailBench, and DataDwarf can generate realistic workload
traces and execute in real environments under overload scenarios. Due to time constraints,
only TailBench was tested.
Several container classification approaches were analyzed, but optimizing overcom-
mitment policies using these container grouping ideas remains a challenging and open
problem. There is no publicly available dataset about containers to enable effective cluster-
ing, and time series data clustering algorithms do not perform well for workloads when
there is variability within the same class. A periodicity-based approach was also explored,
but research indicates that even this approach struggles to differentiate between human
workloads and machine workloads. The only option seems to be creating a model like
Google Autopilot.
The final section of this chapter includes implementation details about data extraction.
Polars and Rayon are the primary tools used in the extraction process. Polars is used for
all trace data, while Rayon is used specifically for the Google Borg dataset. The process of
extracting data involves checking data consistency, using batch processing to speed up
reading, applying a fixed hash to generate the data for reproducible simulation runs, and
managing some special optimizations for each CSP.

Latency is influenced by the accumulation of computational requests. When an ap-
plication handles a computationally intensive request, subsequent requests can quickly
queue up. As processing each request takes time, this leads to increased overall latency.
The faster the server accumulates such requests, the higher the resulting latency.

crucial for designing effective container-centric dynamic overcommitment policies.
7.2
Evaluation of resource underutilization
To effectively evaluate resource waste, it is essential to define metrics that provide
meaningful insights into resource utilization. One fundamental metric is the resource
waste ratio, calculated as the ratio of wasted resources to total resource capacity. This ratio
measures the proportion of unused resources relative to the total available capacity, offering
a clear perspective on resource allocation efficiency within the CSP environment.
This approach analyzes trace data to identify patterns in resource usage inside CSP.
Different types of workloads have distinct resource underutilization, and the defined metric
contributes to understanding the resource efficiency of CSP.
While it is preferable to evaluate each machine individually to determine total re-
source utilization, some datasets lack details on container-to-machine allocation, making
direct machine allocation analysis impossible. Despite this limitation, machine usage can
still be inferred by analyzing container usage and the resource limits assigned to each
container.
The analysis could be more interesting if the dataset included additional information,
such as the number of users accessing the service or the volume of queries per hour.
This data would provide insights into user behavior and allow for the identification of
daily access trends. For example, in low-traffic environments, like personal sites, this can
demonstrate how overcommitment strategies can be effectively implemented. Since this
information is not available in the current dataset, the analysis must focus on the CPU
and memory usage ratios to examine resource underutilization.
For the analysis of resource underutilization in CSP, a seven-day interval is selected
for the Alibaba Trace data. For each day of the week, data points on used and allocated
resources are collected and organized. The x-axis is divided by hour and categorized by
container or machine when machine-level data are available. The Alibaba Trace data do not
provide an exact start time. To prevent initialization issues in the Alibaba Trace data, any
data that have a timestamp below 600 are excluded, and the analysis begins at timestamp
600.

7.3
Comparison between overcommitment policies
The basic overcommitment strategies are tested using the Microsoft Azure trace dataset
(2019). The experiment configuration includes a first-fit scheduling algorithm, sets an
overload handler to “no-limit”, and uses an evaluator to generate the number of machines
used, the violation size, and the violation count. The overcommitment policies that are
compared are machine-centric and container-centric static approaches to CPU resources.
When a container cannot be placed on any machine, a new machine is added to the cluster.
Each machine has 50 units of CPU and 200 units of memory.
The simulation is executed 100 times, each run with 100 containers selected at random,
and the average of the results from the evaluator is calculated. For the reproducibility
of the experiment, each run has the same seed for ChaCha8, and this random number
generator seed ensures the consistency among the different runs.
7.3.1
Machine-centric overcommitment
In the machine-centric approach, the experiment evaluates a static overcommitment
policy where the overcommitment rate is set at 100%, 110%, 120%, and 130%. By observing
how each rate affects resource usage and violation counts, the study aims to characterize
the effectiveness of static overcommitment rates at the machine level.
7.3.2
Container-centric overcommitment
Static
In this approach, the overcommitment rate is determined by the p-th percentile of
container resource usage. Specifically, the experiment evaluates policies using the 90th
percentile, the 95th percentile, the maximum usage values, and a baseline with no over-
commitment.
A key hypothesis is that lowering the percentile, such as from the 95th to the 90th, will
result in a higher violation rate as the resources become more compactly allocated. The
objective is to identify the lowest percentile threshold at which the violation rate remains
negligible, ideally zero. To evaluate this, four distinct policies are compared:
• Overcommitment based on the 90th percentile.
• Overcommitment based on the 95th percentile.
• Overcommitment based on the maximum usage value.
• No overcommitment.

creases from 1.98 to 1.76, which corresponds to an 11% reduction in the number of machines.
This improvement indicates more efficient machine packing. Another observation is that
the machine-centric approach is more prone to violations than the container-centric static
approach.
7.4
Summary
This chapter constitutes the experimental component of the thesis and presents three
experiments designed to understand the cloud environment where resources are over-
committed. When using a simulator, it is important to understand the accuracy of the
model and its effects compared to the real environment. Such accuracy evaluation is not
possible in this study due to the use of historical trace data, which cannot be re-executed
for validation.
The first experiment investigated the relationship between CPU usage and latency. To
examine this relationship, the TailBench benchmark was evaluated using a predefined set
of workloads. The experiment found that when CPU capacity is exceeded and unable to
handle incoming queries, latency increases exponentially. The degree of latency differs
for each workload, suggesting that each container should be analyzed individually when
predicting latency.
The second experiment assessed resource utilization levels in CSP workloads by vi-
sualizing usage patterns for individual resources. This analysis aimed to provide a clear
understanding of how significantly the system is underutilized and to identify potential
benefits and issues that overcommitment can bring to CSP.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The results presented in this thesis are partial because the scope of the project was
too extensive for one person to manage. Implementing each component and extracting
information from time series data on the order of gigabytes to terabytes is time-consuming.
Another issue is the lack of efficient classification and clustering tools in the literature
designed for time series datasets of cloud traces and the absence of detailed container
information needed to categorize workloads. During the experiment design, it was assumed
that workloads could be divided into just two categories, batch tasks and online services,
but this simplification does not capture the complexity of real-world scenarios encountered
in CSP environments.
Different cloud trace datasets were examined and found appropriate for testing over-
commitment policies. The Google Borg dataset is particularly useful for evaluating how
containers are allocated to machines, while the Microsoft Azure dataset is better suited for
analyzing overcommitment between batch tasks and online services. These datasets, in-
cluding the Alibaba dataset, provide valuable insights into CSP’s resource utilization.
A simulator was developed to allow users to test data from cloud trace datasets and try
different overcommitment policies before deploying the workloads in a production environ-
ment. The simulator also includes tools for evaluating overload mechanisms, scheduling
methods, and performance metrics. Additionally, it supports the allocation of machines and
containers in multiple sizes, enabling users to simulate different heterogeneous resource
configurations. In the future, time-bound overcommitment is going to be implemented,
but it currently remains incomplete as it needs to develop a better resource history for
tracking execution times, and also finish the overload mechanism.
Overcommitment has proven to be effective in optimizing resource usage in the cloud.
Users often overestimate their resource requirements, resulting in unused resources that
can be reallocated to other containers. Leveraging these unused resources usually presents
minimal challenges, especially for applications that do not have strict latency or completion
time requirements.
The effectiveness of memory and CPU overcommitment depends largely on the type
of workload. Overcommitting resources may not be ideal for CPU-intensive containers
that experience frequent spikes or for latency-sensitive workloads, but it can be very

54
8 | CONCLUSION
beneficial for containers that consistently use low resources and experience only occasional
spikes.
In a Kubernetes cluster, unused resources should be resized to match the containers’
actual needs. Fine-tuning resource limits helps reduce costs and align the infrastructure
with current demand levels (Google Cloud n.d.).
Reducing the QoS for lower-priority containers during overcommitment can lead to
container termination, slowdowns, or unresponsiveness. Determining an optimal over-
commitment policy is challenging because it requires assessing each container to evaluate
the overall impact on system performance.
If the cluster is not overcommitted, but the cluster manager wants to try out over-
committing machines or containers, a straightforward strategy is to start with a fixed
overcommitment rate and monitor system performance. If operations remain smooth,
gradually increase the rate, but if issues emerge, reduce the rate. Continue this iterative
process until an optimal overcommitment rate is reached.
To implement overcommitment on a per-container basis, a dynamic strategy can be
adopted. For each container, the overcommitment rate is determined based on the p-
th percentile of its workload type. The violation rate is then monitored to ensure that
performance remains within predefined acceptable limits.
A potential direction for implementing automatic overcommitment is to adopt con-
cepts from Google Autopilot. This approach creates a resource allocation model based on
factors such as underrun, overrun, safe margins, and decay rates. It applies reinforcement
learning-like techniques to find the most suitable model for each container’s resource
usage. By directly determining the optimal allocation, this approach avoids clustering
algorithms.
While the clustering-based approach relies on clustering features, the lack of detailed
container information in publicly available datasets makes replicating the strategy used
by Microsoft Resource Central infeasible.

can extend the understanding of overcommitment and provide opportunities to explore
certain topics in greater depth. Additionally, the existing concepts presented in this thesis
could also be further refined and enhanced, building on the progress made so far.

Comparison of different time windows in resource
history
Cloud environments are highly dynamic, with tasks constantly changing. Relying on
long-term historical data for calculations may introduce outdated information, potentially
resulting in suboptimal overcommitment decisions for specific workload types.
8.1.3
Comparison among scheduling algorithms
Several scheduling algorithms can be employed when allocating containers to machines,
with the goal of efficiently packing containers to minimize the number of machines
required. Research indicates that the dot-product algorithm performs slightly better than
others, although there are scenarios where alternative algorithms may outperform it
(Panigrahy et al., 2011). There is also a dataset specifically designed for vector bin packing,
such as the Microsoft Bin-packing dataset, that could be used to test and validate these
algorithms.
8.1.4
Comparison among overload handling
The overload handling experiment in the Google Autopilot paper (Rzadca et al., 2020)
evaluated OOM under soft-limit and hard-limit memory mechanisms. The results showed
significant differences in outcomes related to out-of-memory (OOM) events. This overload
handler could be extended to include live migration for handling overload in other types
of overload scenarios.
8.1.5
Automation of overcommitment policies
Developing built-in automated overcommitment policies for Kubernetes clusters could
eliminate the need for administrators to manually configure node resource limits, allow-
ing them to focus on other tasks. A similar approach is employed in Google Autopilot,
which tests several parameters, such as overrun limit, underrun limit, safety margins,
and decay rates, combined with a model evaluation technique inspired by reinforcement
learning.
8.1.6
Clustering-based overcommitment policies
Clustering time series data to associate patterns with container behavior presents
significant challenges, largely due to the limited details available in trace data. Although
patterns in the time series can be identified, effectively clustering these patterns to analyze
overcommitment has proven difficult. Several clustering algorithms have been evaluated,
but the literature indicates low effectiveness, primarily due to the dataset variability within
the same workload types.

8 | CONCLUSION
A key limitation of public datasets is the lack of detailed container information, such
as machine and operating system configurations, as well as container-level information.
The lack of data restricts accurate analysis and modeling of container behavior.
8.1.7
Machine learning in overcommitment policies
Machine learning can play a crucial role in selecting among various overcommitment
policies or determining appropriate overcommitment rates. The following three exam-
ples from the literature illustrate how machine learning techniques can be applied to
overcommitment:
Microsoft Resource Central extracts information from containers to generate predictive
models using algorithms such as Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost).
These models infer container utilization brackets, and based on the inferred utilization, an
overcommitment policy rate is determined (Cortez et al., 2017). However, the absence
of container information, as noted earlier regarding the limitations of the public dataset,
makes this method non-reproducible.
Google Autopilot leverages machine learning techniques to develop a model that fits
the container. The system incorporates factors such as underrun, overrun, safety net, and
decay rate to determine which model is most appropriate to apply. Autopilot adopts a
model similar to reinforcement learning (RL), selecting the model that achieves the highest
reward based on these criteria (Rzadca et al., 2020).
Unlike Google Autopilot, chance-constrained multi-agent reinforcement learning
(C2MARL) uses a specific framework to determine overcommitment rates among multiple
agents (Sheng et al., 2023). This framework employs relaxation techniques to find an
approximate solution through linear programming constraints, thereby generating multi-
agent coordination.
Reinforcement learning is challenging due to the complexity of designing effective
reward and penalty systems for agent actions. Another challenge is balancing exploration
and exploitation to ensure that the agent consistently learns optimal actions, which
complicates policy development using reinforcement learning.
