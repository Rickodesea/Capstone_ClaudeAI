Abstract—One of the most challenging problems in the pop-
ular orchestration framework Kubernetes is assigning sufﬁcient
resources to containers to operate at a required level while also
avoiding excessive resource allocation which can delay other
jobs in the cluster. A variety of heuristic approaches have been
proposed to tackle this problem but these require considerable
manual adjustments which can be laborious. Reinforcement
learning approaches have been proposed to address this issue but
these proposals do not consider the energy consumption of the
cluster. This is an important component of the problem due to the
commitments of large cloud operators to carbon neutrality. We
have proposed a system called Smart-Kube to achieve a target
utilization on nodes while maintaining energy consumption at
a reasonable level. An experimental framework is designed on
top of real-world Kubernetes clusters and real-world traces of
container jobs are used to evaluate the framework. Experimental
results show that Smart-Kube can approach the target utilization
and reduce energy consumption in a variety of ways depending
on the preferences of the cluster operator for a variety of cluster
sizes.

I. INTRODUCTION
Recently containerization has become the dominant virtu-
alization technology used in cloud computing platforms [1].
The management of diverse containers running in the cloud is
a challenging problem and several orchestration frameworks
have been developed to address these challenges. One of
the most popular container orchestration frameworks is Ku-
bernetes which is an open-source framework developed by
Google.
One of the most important components of the orchestration
framework is resource management which attempts to fairly
distribute jobs to computing nodes in order to make sure that
they function at a required level while also ensuring that the
minimal number of physical nodes are utilized for serving
containerized workloads. A variety of heuristic approaches for
resource management in cloud platforms have been developed
including fair scheduling [2], ﬁrst ﬁt [3] and simple packing
strategies [4]. Several meta-heuristic approaches have also
been proposed including genetic algorithms [5] and ant colony
algorithms [6]. The success of these approaches is heavily
dependent on the job scheduling algorithm utilized as the
resources required will depend on the number of jobs being
directed to the Kubernetes nodes. Thus, the tuning of these
algorithms can be a laborious process requiring considerable
expertise. There have, however, been several proposals for
using reinforcement learning in these platforms to avoid this
with DeepRM [7] and Decima [8] being two notable proposals
in this area.
While there has been considerable work in job scheduling
for efﬁcient job execution on container clusters, there has
been little work in energy aware scheduling algorithms for
Kubernetes clusters. Recently, however, large cloud service
providers such as Microsoft and Amazon have increased their
focus on reducing their environmental impact by committing
to carbon neutrality by 2030 and 2040 respectively [9], [10].
Scheduling algorithms will likely need to consider the energy
consumption of the container clusters due to increasing pres-
sure from government organizations [11]. Thus, we propose
the Smart-Kube system that considers both overall energy
consumption and the load distribution of container clusters
to provide good performance for its containers and a reduced
environmental impact.
The main contributions of this research work are:
• We present the Smart-Kube system that integrates with
Kubernetes to provide a scheduling service that dis-
tributes load evenly between container clusters while
considering energy consumption. Smart-Kube models the
problem of job scheduling while maintaining a reasonable
energy consumption as an optimization problem and
solves it using a reinforcement learning algorithm.
• We present a dataset of jobs traces that heavily utilizes
containers for processing machine learning jobs. Our
analysis shows that most Kubernetes pods are highly
under utilized in terms of CPU and memory usage. This
highlights the importance of scheduling algorithms that
use actual resource usage as well as requested resources
in scheduling algorithms. While the actual resource usage
of jobs is not known at scheduling time Smart-Kube uses
current job usage to schedule incoming jobs.
• We examine the performance of Smart-Kube with dif-
ferent reward weights on different cluster sizes to assess
the trade-off between the two aforementioned objectives

II. MOTIVATING SCENARIO
The increasing demand for scalable and efﬁcient computing
systems has resulted in the widespread adoption of cloud
computing and container technologies, such as Kubernetes [1].
However, with the growing complexity of these systems, the
management of their resources and allocation of jobs in a fair
and efﬁcient manner has become increasingly challenging [1].
Job scheduling in Kubernetes involves allocating compu-
tational resources, such as computing nodes, to different jobs
running in the cluster. The primary objective of job scheduling
is to allocate the resources in a way that balances resource uti-
lization, job execution time, and cost efﬁciency while meeting
the resource requirements of each job. This task becomes par-
ticularly challenging in large-scale Kubernetes clusters, where
numerous jobs contend for the same resources. The scheduler
must ensure that each job is assigned to an appropriate node,
based on its resource requirements and the current state of
the cluster. The scheduler must also continuously monitor
the cluster, making adjustments as necessary to ensure that
resources are allocated optimally and that jobs are running
smoothly.

III. RELATED WORKS
Reinforcement learning has been used for several job
scheduling applications in cloud platforms. Mao et al. pro-
posed DeepRM which uses reinforcement learning to schedule
jobs. The resource requirements of the job are modeled as
an image and this is used by the scheduling algorithm to
minimize delays to the job completion time [7]. Mao et al.
also presented Decima which uses reinforcement learning to
schedule tasks with dependencies in Spark clusters. The tasks
are modeled as a graph and graph convolutional networks
as well as reinforcement learning are utilized to schedule
the jobs [8]. Huang et al. proposed RLSK which utilizes
reinforcement learning to balance resource utilization between
container clusters [1]. Vrbaski et al. presented a system that
uses reinforcement learning to schedule microservices while
considering deadlines, cost and cluster utilization [12]. There
have also been scheduling proposals on container platforms
for niche applications such as HPKS proposed by Shi et al.
which supports blockchain applications on container platforms
[13]. Our work differs from these works in two principal ways.
Firstly, these works do not consider the energy consumption
of the clusters in their optimization formulations. Our work
attempts to balance the performance of the jobs running on the
cluster while also considering the energy consumption of the
container clusters. Secondly, most of the results of these works
are simulation based and do not experimentally evaluate these
works on real cloud platforms. Our work has been integrated
with Kubernetes and has been deployed on the Google Cloud
Platform as part of our evaluation.
Minimizing the energy consumption of cloud computing
components has been examined in numerous recent works.
S´egu´ela et al. proposed a system for minimizing the energy
consumption of data storage by adapting the data replication
strategy [14]. Nordlund et al. proposed EALA which utilizes
memory disaggregation on virtual machine platforms with
a scheduling algorithm to reduce energy consumption [15].
Ghafouri et al. [16], [17] proposed an energy and mobility
aware framework for Kubernetes edge clusters using rein-
forcement learning. Zhao et al. presented a geographical load
balancing system for large clouds that shifts load based upon
the availability of renewable energy [18]. Salmani et al. [19]
presented an adaptive framework for minimizing cost and

IV. PROBLEM FORMULATION
This section outlines the methodology employed in our
study to address the Kubernetes job scheduling problem,
including the representative structure, the optimization formu-
lation, and the cost function.
A. Problem Representation
We represent this problem by modeling the environment
states of the system and possible agent actions. Our represen-
tations are inspired by the previous work in this ﬁeld [1], [7],
[8], [16], [20].
• States: The states are represented by a one-dimensional
vector that captures the current resource usage of each
node as well as the characteristics of jobs waiting to be
scheduled. The occupancy ratios of a node’s resources
indicate the overall resource usage of that node at a
particular time. Similarly, the state of waiting jobs can be
depicted by the required resource ratios from the node.
To facilitate the representation of the states of nodes and
jobs as an array of ratios between 0 and 1, we adopt the
following assumptions:
– All nodes have the same number (types) of resources;
– Resources of the same type have consistent sizes
across all nodes;
– Time is assumed to be discrete and jobs arrive
discretely in the system.
Indeed, these simpliﬁcations may not always align with
real-world scenarios. However, they can signiﬁcantly re-
duce the complexity and boost the convergence of the
scheduler.
• Actions: An action can be characterized as the index
of the node to which each job is assigned. The action
represented by the index 0 can be used to temporarily
postpone that job for a random time interval rather than
attempting to schedule it immediately when the agents
determine that the current system is not suitable for
scheduling the job (e.g. due to high nodes workload).
This allows the job to be delayed until a more optimal
system state is reached.

C. Cost Function
We propose a cost-aware approach for fair job scheduling
in Kubernetes clusters that is based on two key formulations:
target utilization and minimum nodes in use. The target uti-
lization formulation is used to ensure that resources are being
used efﬁciently, while the minimum nodes in use formulation
P(t) is used to minimize the number of active nodes and thus
reduce costs.
Target utilization, G(t), is a key aspect of our proposed
approach for fair job scheduling in Kubernetes clusters. It
combines maximizing overall utilization, U(t), and even
load distribution function, V (t), to reduce complexity in the
scheduler’s decision-making process, leading to more efﬁcient
resource usage and cost reduction for the cluster. Additionally,
this combination of factors helps with the training process, as
it needs to be calculated multiple times to reach convergence.
We deﬁne the target utilization value as a hyperparameter that
deﬁnes the maximum allowed load (percentage of resource
utilization) in a cluster that aims to bring the nodes’ utilization
Un(t) as close as possible to this deﬁned value, ensuring both
high overall utilization and low utilization imbalance. Guide-
lines from the US Government indicate that the utilization
level should be between 55% and 75% depending on the
operator [11]. Target utilization at time t and given the target

Agent In our experimentation, we utilized the Deep Q-
Network Neural Network (DQN) as the agent. DQN is a
model-free off-policy reinforcement learning algorithm [24].
It extends the Q-Learning algorithm by employing a deep
neural network to estimate the Q-value function. The Q-value
represents the expected total reward that the agent will receive
from a speciﬁc state-action pair.
To enhance the learning process, DQN employs a replay
buffer to store states, actions, rewards, and next states. This
buffer allows the agent to learn from a diverse set of experi-
ences, ultimately improving learning efﬁciency.
The training of the DQN algorithm involves minimizing the
mean-squared error between the predicted Q-values and the
actual Q-values. The training targets are computed using the
Bellman equation, which updates the DQN Neural Network
weights by minimizing the following loss function:

To balance exploration and exploitation, DQN utilizes an
epsilon-greedy policy. This policy enables the agent to choose
a random action with probability ϵ and the action with the

the value of ϵ gradually decreases, shifting the agent’s focus
from exploration to exploitation as learning progresses.

slack [25]. It is deﬁned as the fraction of requested resources
that are not used over a speciﬁc time period, usually a
relatively long period like a day. We have also calculated
the slack values over the entire time period of the dataset.
It was found to be 94% slack for memory and 68% for CPU.
This highlights that considerable efﬁciencies can be achieved
by considering cluster utilization instead of request sizes and
powering off physical infrastructure that is not needed. While
this does increase the risk of Out of Memory errors and CPU
throttling the dataset shows that these events would be rare.
An example of the CPU and memory usage of a container is
shown in Figure 5. From the ﬁgure, we can see that the usage
is considerably lower than the request limit. It never exceeds
40% in the case of CPU and 20% in the case of memory. It is
also interesting to note that usage is relatively stable over the
lifetime of the container. This highlights the minimal risk in
using resource usage as well as request limits in the scheduling
algorithm. While this is of course only one container our
analysis shows that the majority of containers show similar
usage patterns. The ratio between the utilization and limit
varies somewhat but large dynamic changes in resource usage
were only found in a minority of the container traces. Thus,
Smart-Kube uses resource usage as well as resource requests
when scheduling to attempt to maximize potential energy
savings.

Kubernetes. All containers associated with a pod will always
be co-located on the same cluster node. Pods are ephemeral
objects and will be replaced and rescheduled during their
lifetime. Kubernetes will use the concept of services to build
a consistent representation of the networking information of
the Kubernetes pods networking to enable access. The cluster
is monitored and controlled using the Kubernetes API server.
The API is essentially a REST server and there are numerous
tools like the Kubernetes CLI named kubectl or other client
APIs available in multiple languages. Our system utilizes the
python client API to monitor and control the clusters.
Our deﬁnition of Jobs. Kubernetes schedules jobs as
pods at the beginning of their lifetime and thus, we deﬁne
jobs as the pods required to execute the workload of the
Arabesque dataset described in Section VI. These pods will
have diverse resource requirements and utilization proﬁles. For
experimental purposes, we limit the deﬁnition of a job to a
single pod containing one single container inside it with a
Flask Python app for generating load on the container CPU
and RAM. The Flask Python app is exposed to the outside
world using Kubernetes’ API.
Load Generation Module. To emulate artiﬁcial load on
the jobs, we have used a model with two containerized Flask
applications. One of them called the utilization server is
deployed as a single Flask application in one arbitrary server
outside the cluster and the other one is an application running
on all the pods. At each timestamp, the utilization server
sends the resource usage of each application to them and the
applications on the services put the load on the CPU and RAM
of the application using a Linux tool named stress-ng.
Energy saving mechanism. One of the aims of our ap-
proach is to minimize the number of Kuberntes nodes available
in the cluster. We have not implemented a mechanism for
changing the power state of the physical machine as we are
using virtual machines in our experiment. The Kubernetes
pods could be drained from the cluster and the physical
host could be powered off to achieve this for a real-world
implementation.
Training. It is costly and time consuming to train on a
physical cluster. Several timesteps are required to train the RL
agents in the environment. Thus, it is impractical to execute
the training on a physical cluster. We have implemented a
simulator that mimics the dynamics of the real-world cluster
during training to address this issue. The trained agent is
then utilized outside the box in the real-world cluster for the
experimental evaluation.
Complete System. As it can be seen in Figure 6, our
design consists of three main parts. The ﬁrst part is the
Kubernetes cluster containing our Kubernetes nodes and jobs.

VIII. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
We used the Google GKE service to deploy our Kubernetes
cluster. Due to our computational budget, we performed the
experiments on a cluster that contained six, eight and ten
Kubernetes nodes. All the nodes were of n2-highcpu-8 type of
the GKE platform with eight cores and 8 GB of Memory. The
RL agents were trained on a synthetic dataset whose contents
are summarized in Table I. We choose to use a synthetic
dataset to allow us to evaluate the general performance of the
algorithm for different datasets. For the testing experiments,
jobs are selected randomly from the Arabesque dataset and
a new job is scheduled every thirty seconds. A total of 2240
jobs were executed for each variation of the testing phase.
We evaluated the result in terms of the joint energy saving
and utilization balancing objectives. We evaluate the perfor-
mance of the algorithm using three different variations of the
algorithm namely “Consolidation” which prioritizes the energy
saving objective, “Fairness” which prioritizes the utilization
balancing objective and “Balance” which prioritizes both re-
wards equally. For the “Consolidation” variation the weights
are set to (w1 = 0, w2 = 1). For the “Fairness” variation the
weights were set to (w1 = 1, w2 = 0). For the “Balance”
variation the weights were set to (w1 = 0.5, w2 = 0.5). We
also evaluate the performance of the algorithm on different
cluster sizes to evaluate how scale affects the performance of
the algorithm. We utilize a six, eight and ten node cluster in
our experiments.
During the training phase of the algorithm, we observed that
all cluster sizes converge to values that prioritize the weights
of the rewards. This is illustrated in Figure 7 and Figure 8.

IX. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
In this paper, we presented a deep reinforcement learning
solution for reducing energy consumption in containerized
clusters while maintaining a target utilization between the
nodes in the cluster. Our results suggest that the trade-off
between energy consumption and achieving target utilization
can be achieved in a variety of ways by adjusting the reward
weights while also improving performance in other areas.
Although our proposed Smart-Kube job scheduler has shown
promising results, there is still scope for future work to
improve its performance and make it more applicable in real-
world scenarios.
Utilizing hierarchical and multi-agent RL can be another
direction to scale up and achieve better results in more
complex environments. This is because these approaches can
enable the handling of large numbers of jobs and more
distributed data centers. Re-scheduling of existing services
can be an alternative future research direction. By evaluating

the current state of the environment, Smart-Kube can predict
the best time to re-schedule existing services. The selection
of optimal values for the parameters and hyperparameters
of the learning models was a challenging task that we had
to tackle through our knowledge and experience. Future work
can explore new techniques such as meta-heuristics, hyper-
heuristics, or other approaches that can assist in setting the
best parameter values for the algorithm.
