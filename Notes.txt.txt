  Instructor's two scenarios — brief thoughts (not implementing)

  1. Job affinity — tenant jobs must re-run on the same node

  This is called gang scheduling or co-location constraint in the literature. The standard LP formulation adds binary "node occupancy" variables $y_{g,n}$ per affinity group $g$ and node
  $n$, then adds linking constraints $x_{j,n} \leq y_{g(j),n}$ and $\sum_n y_{g,n} = 1$. It keeps groups together without breaking the MILP structure, just adds variables.

  References to look at:
  - Kubernetes https://kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/scheduling-eviction/assign-pod-node/
  - "Borg, Omega, and Kubernetes" (Google, 2016) — §5 discusses co-location policies
  - Scheduling in large-scale distributed systems literature under "gang scheduling"

  2. Tenant exclusivity — no two tenants share a node

  This is a classic bin packing with incompatibility problem. Model it with a binary variable $y_{t,n} = 1$ if tenant $t$ occupies node $n$, then add $\sum_t y_{t,n} \leq 1$ per node and
  $x_{jn} \leq y_{t(j),n}$. Fair warning: this constraint severely hurts utilization — a node can only serve one tenant at a time, so most nodes will sit idle waiting for the right
  tenant's jobs.

  References:
  - Kubernetes https://kubernetes.io/docs/concepts/scheduling-eviction/taint-and-toleration/ — the practical Kubernetes mechanism
  - "Multi-tenant isolation in cloud computing" — search this phrase in IEEE/ACM DL
  - Chaudhari 2025 paper you already have may touch on isolation strategies
