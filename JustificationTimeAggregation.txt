## Short Jobs Are Still Captured

From **Chen et al. (2010), Section 4.1.2:**

> *"Task durations show bimodal behavior. Job Types 0, 1, 2 have mostly short tasks with a few long tasks."*

Short jobs exist. Your aggregation method still captures them.

**Example:**

| Job | Start Time | End Time | Duration | What happens |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Job A | 10:05 | 10:12 | 7 minutes | Fully inside Hour 1. Captured. |
| Job B | 10:55 | 11:08 | 13 minutes | Spans Hour 1 and Hour 2. Captured in both. |
| Job C | 10:10 | 10:10 | 0 minutes? | Does not exist. Every job has duration. |

**Your 1-hour window captures:**
- The maximum CPU usage during the job's short lifetime
- The maximum total memory usage during the job's short lifetime
- When the job ran (which hour bucket)

From **Google trace documentation v3:**

> *"The measurement windows are typically 5 minutes (300s) long, although may be shorter if the instance starts, stops, or is updated within that time period."*

Even the raw trace truncates windows for short-running instances. Google does not have infinite granularity either.

## What Happens To A 5-Minute Job In 1-Hour Aggregation

**Scenario:** Job runs from 10:05 to 10:10 (5 minutes).

| Time Window | Job Status | CPU MAX captured? | MEM MAX captured? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Hour 1 (10:00-11:00) | Running | YES (0.8) | YES (4GB) |
| Hour 2 (11:00-12:00) | Not running | N/A | N/A |

**You lose nothing.** The peak values are still in Hour 1.

**What you lose:** The exact minute (10:07) when the spike happened. But you do not need that for max usage prediction.

## The Only Potential "Loss" – And Why It Does Not Matter

**What you might miss:** If a job starts at 10:55 and ends at 11:05, its peak could be split across two hours.

| Time Window | CPU MAX |
| :--- | :--- |
| Hour 1 (10:00-11:00) | 0.6 (from 10:55-11:00) |
| Hour 2 (11:00-12:00) | 0.8 (from 11:00-11:05) |

**Your max across hours = 0.8.** This is correct. The job's true peak (0.8) is still captured. You just do not know it happened at 11:02 instead of 10:58.

From **Borg paper (Verma et al., 2015), Section 5.5:**

> *"The initial reservation is set equal to the resource request (the limit); after 300s, to allow for startup transients, it decays slowly towards the actual usage."*

Borg itself waits 5 minutes (300 seconds) before adjusting reservations. They acknowledge that startup transients exist. Your 1-hour window is still capturing the relevant peak.

## Comparison Of Granularities For Short Jobs

| Job Duration | 5-minute granularity | 1-hour granularity | Information Lost |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 5 minutes | 1 data point | 1 data point (in that hour) | None for max value |
| 15 minutes | 3 data points | 1 data point (max of the 3) | The progression within the hour |
| 30 minutes | 6 data points | 1 data point (max of the 6) | The progression within the hour |
| 2 hours | 24 data points | 2 data points | Some temporal resolution |

**For max usage prediction:** You only need the peak, not the progression. The peak survives aggregation.

From **Kang (2022), Section 3.3.1:**

> *"Both CPU and memory resource in usage and allocation follow an extreme heavy-tailed power-law Pareto distribution."*

Most jobs are "mice" (small/short). They will be captured entirely within a single hour bucket.
